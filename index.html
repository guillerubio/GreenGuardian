Abstract

The Green Guardian project addresses substantial economic losses in the houseplant industry, where a significant portion of plants perish annually due to neglect. This IoT device simplifies plant care to a near-autonomous level, focusing primarily on precise soil humidity management through its integrated watering system. Once installed, Green Guardian monitors soil moisture and adjusts watering schedules accordingly, ensuring plants receive exactly what they need without risk of over or underwatering. It also monitors environmental factors such as light and temperature and advises owners when adjustments are needed to maintain optimal conditions. Powered by an energy-harvesting system, the device minimizes its environmental footprint and operates independently of external power sources. With its cloud-connected capabilities, Green Guardian offers users remote monitoring and minimal interaction, making it an ideal solution for ensuring plant health with little to no effort. This device not only promises to extend the life of houseplants but also to transform plant care into a hassle-free, sustainable routine.

Problem Definition

Americans invest approximately $16 billion annually in houseplants. Sadly, it is estimated that nearly half of these plants die due to improper care, which indicates a substantial gap in the consumer knowledge or available technology aiding in plant maintenance.

This situation presents a significant opportunity for innovation in the home gardening industry, particularly through the development of smart technologies that can reduce plant mortality rates and enhance user experience.

Related Work

Various researchers have explored the design of IoT-based smart plant monitoring systems. Judika et al. investigated the potential benefits of such a system in monitoring Chili plants in Indonesia [2]. Their system is capable of monitoring the moisture, pH, and salinity of the soil and autonomously adjusting these parameters using water, pH up/down liquid, and AB mix, respectively [2]. While the system functions effectively in the field, it is specifically designed to cater to a single type of plant. Kush et al. endeavored to broaden the scope of plant monitoring by introducing a smart pot [1]. However, this approach is marred by two significant flaws: users are required to replace their existing pots with smart pots, and the pot relies on a user to regularly charge its battery. Antti et al. devised an AI system capable of monitoring plant health, yet it relies on human intervention for plant care based on the interpreted data [3]. The limitations of previous research underscore the importance of developing a self-sufficient autonomous plant monitoring system.

Sensor Modalities

For monitoring plant health, there are various features that need to be observed and maintained, such as soil health through pH and moisture content sensing, amount of ambient light and humidity. These parameters can be conveniently calculated using IoT sensors, and are paramount for effective monitoring. The collected data assists in making decisions about irrigation, soil nutrition, and determining which plant types to grow. The pH sensor serves the purpose of monitoring the nutrient levels necessary for the growth of a plant [10]. The soil moisture sensor functions to assess the moisture content within the soil, mitigating the risk of plant desiccation due to inadequate watering practices [1,2]. Additionally, the monitoring of air temperature and humidity, often facilitated by the DHT11 sensor, stands as another crucial aspect of plant monitoring systems [1, 2, 10].

Energy Harvesting

Ideally, IoT systems should be self-sustainable and functional for a long period of time. Especially for our approach to a self-contained plant monitoring system that requires minimal to no human interference, we would like to ensure a longer network lifetime. With this is mind, using batteries as the primary or only power source poses several challenges, as highlighted in [7]: (i) finite lifetime, (ii) depletion and leakage issues, (iii) expensive maintenance and replacement costs, (iv) effects due to temperature, (v) impact of battery chemicals on surrounding environment, (vi) problems in maintenance and replacement due to IoT systems deployment in inaccessible or remote locations. Therefore, we have to come up with energy harvesting (EH) techniques to provide secondary power sources or even replace batteries altogether. Work by T. Sanislav et al. extensively investigates the major energy sources that can be used for EH and subsequent models for this purpose, choosing either an immediate Harvest-Use or Harvest-Store-Use model [8]. The work by T. Sanislav evaluated a host of EH sources, encompassing ambient, human, organic, mechanical, and hybrid sources. Criteria for evaluation included (i) technology, (ii) power density, (iii) controllability, (iv) predictability, and (v) availability. Based on their findings, solar energy is easily and naturally available, and delivers the highest power density of approximately 15 mW/cm2, as compared to various other energy harvesting techniques [9]. Limitations of solar energy as EH source include (i) uncontrollability, (ii) unavailability during night, and (iii) effects on weather, season, etc. However, these limitations can be effectively mitigated by utilizing the predictability of such effects. As light, or sunlight is one of the primary requirements for plant growth, utilizing solar energy to both recharge and power our IoT system is a logical choice. Along with selecting a viable energy source, we also need to harvest energy in a way that suits our systemâ€™s needs and components. Primarily, we should focus on the amount of energy generated and consumed. In case of sources such as solar energy which generates a high-output voltage, far more than the requirement of our low-power IoT system, it is wise to store energy for later use, for eg. during night, or when availability of solar energy is dampened due to weather or seasonal variations. Due to its nature as an uncontrollable but predictable resource, solar energy lends itself to a harvest-store-use model for uninterrupted operation [12], [13]. Several approaches have been explored to maximize data transmission rate [14], [15] through optimal scheduling schemes for power management. In [7], the authors have designed a solar-powered environmental BLE beacon, that allowed stand-alone operation, with the solar cells being capable of charging the accumulator within a single day with good weather. A similar approach replacing solar with RF showed lesser amounts of scavenged energy.

Cloud computing

Cloud connectivity constitutes a crucial aspect of plant monitoring systems. Wi-Fi stands as the predominant interface for network connectivity [2,3]. Nevertheless, Bluetooth, particularly its variant Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), consumes less power and serves as an optimal choice for connecting to smartphones [4]. Ethernet, as a wired connectivity option, offers a reliable connection [1]. The communication of plant health data to users commonly occurs through smartphone apps [1]. Alternatively, websites or web apps, which are platform-agnostic, present another viable option [2, 3].

Irrigation Techniques

Effective irrigation techniques are essential for maintaining plants alive, as precise delivery of water to the roots profoundly impacts their overall health and development. Some irrigation systems designed for efficiency and minimal human intervention have been designed in the past. Judika et al. discuss automated sprinkler and monitoring systems specifically designed for chili plants in urban settings. These systems use a combination of soil moisture sensors and Arduino controllers to regulate watering based on the soil's moisture content. Sensor fusion Sensor fusion is an essential component for plant monitoring. It is necessary to integrate data from various sources such as moisture, humidity, light and temperature to ensure precise soil humidity management and optimal plant health. Previous research by Misra et al. offers examples into how sensor fusion is able to enhance IoT functionality by combining multiple sensor inputs to achieve more accurate, robust and reliable models of the physical environment. Applying sensor fusion is important, as the results of efficient sensor fusion are almost always better than those obtained from the interpretation of data from individual sensors [5]. However, there are many challenges to implementing sensor fusion within the constraints of IoT. Handling voluminous and heterogeneous data efficiently, ensuring real-time processing and maintaining scalability, as discussed by Krishnamurthi et al. Additionally, research focusing specifically on sensor fusion for houseplant environments remains scarce, underscoring a significant gap in current studies.

Technical Approach: Details

The project proposes the creation of the Green Guardian, an IoT device designed to simplify plant care to a near-autonomous level. This device will manage watering schedules based on soil humidity data it collects, adjusting water delivery to the specific needs of each plant.

Key components of the device include sensors for soil moisture, ambient light, and temperature, a water delivery system, and a power management unit featuring solar energy harvesting. This integrated system ensures that plants receive optimal care without requiring constant human intervention.

Product Survey - Preliminary Data

A survey was conducted with 119 participants to gauge potential user interest in the Green Guardian. Questions aimed to assess how often individuals interact with their plants and their interest in a self-sustaining plant care system.

The survey results for the Green Guardian product reveal promising insights into the market's readiness and potential interest in an autonomous plant care solution. A majority of respondents interact with their plants frequently (daily or several times a week), indicating an existing commitment to plant care that the Green Guardian could support and enhance. Additionally, a significant number of respondents reported having plants that died prematurely, suggesting a prevalent challenge in maintaining plant health that the Green Guardian aims to address. Importantly, the strong inclination towards a self-contained watering system, with a substantial majority expressing interest, underscores the demand for such innovative solutions. This interest, coupled with respondents' willingness to invest in the price range of $20 to $60, positions the Green Guardian as a viable product in the houseplant care market.
